The Impossible Problem

1 Nov 2020

My dad has lots of folders. One of them have interesting problems. Each summer he shows me a couple of them. Last year he showed me "The Impossible problem":

This beautiful problem, which I call "impossible" because it seems to lack sufficient information for a solution, began making the rounds of mathematics meetings a year or so ago. I do not know its origin. Mel Stover of Winnipeg was the first to call it to my attention.

Two numbers (not necessarily different) are chosen from the range of possible integers greater than 1 and not greater than 100. Only the rum of the two numbers is given to mathematiciean S. Only the product of the two is given to mathematician P.

On the telephone S says to P: "I see no way you can determine my sum."

An hour later P calls back to say: "I know your sum."

Later S calls P again to report: "Now I know your product."

What are the two numbers?

I like this problem because we have to start with what we don't know. Everything interesting start with not knowing.

Solution

(1) For S to make the first phone call, the sum must not be possible to create from two numbers that are both prime. If both numbers could be primes, P would've been able to determine the sum.

For example, the sum cannot be 10. The sum 10 can be created by 3 and 7 and they are both prime. The product 21 would determine both numbers and and P could easily determine the numbers and the sum.

The sum could be 11. There is no way to create that sum from two numbers that are both prime.
