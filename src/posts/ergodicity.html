I'm not that confident about the meanings and implications of ergodicity. But the concept is sufficiently counter-intuitive to make it interesting to me.

In this article I'll try to explain ergodicity based on a simple game:

  1. You have some amount of wealth.
  2. A fair coin is tossed.
  3. On heads, your wealth increases 50%.
  4. On tails, your wealth decreases 40%.

Ensemble Average

First, let's see what happens on average when many players participate in the game. An average player should gain 5% wealth on each round (on each coin toss):
EA = (1.5 + 0.6) / 2 = 1.05

Now let's take the perspective of an individual player. An individual player should, on average, loose 5% wealth on each round:
TA = (1.5 * 0.6) / 2 = 0.95

Defining Ergodicity

Ergodicity have different meanings in different contexts, but here's a commonly used defintion for our case:
A process is ergodic if the ensemble average is equal to the time average.

Based on this definition, we can conclude that our game is non-ergodic â€” the ensemble average is not equal to the time average.

What I find counter-intuitive is that sometimes it's rational for a group (an ensemble) to participate in a game, but at the same time, irrational for an individual player. How can this be? I understand the math, but on an emotional level, it seems paradoxical.

The Kelly Fraction

To end this article, I'd like to take the opportunity to explain the "Kelly Fraction".

The Kelly Fraction is used in finance and investment. It's rather abstract and typically difficult to understand, but based on our understanding of ergodicity from this article, I believe we can reach a simple description.

Let's slightly modify the game so that a player can choose the amount to bet on each round. In this new scenario, it's in fact possible for an individual player to "get access" to some of the ensemble gains. The optimal amount to bet is calculated by the Kelly Fraction:
KF = P/A - Q/B

P = Probabilty of win
Q = Probability of loss
A = Fraction lost on loss
B = Fraction gained on win

For our game, the Kelly Fraction is:
KF = 0.5/0.4 - 0.5/0.5 = 0.25
